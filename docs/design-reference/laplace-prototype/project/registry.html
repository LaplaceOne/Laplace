Permissionless protocol.
Legible trust.

The Laplace core never whitelists — a maker can target any criterion program. So trust can't live in the protocol; it lives here, in the open: every criterion and SP1 guest carries a trust tier, its audit and build provenance, and its track record. Nothing is hidden, and friction scales with risk.

The trust model

What's automatic,
what's on you.

The protocol guarantees the binding cryptographically. Whether a criterion's logic is honest is a human judgment — the registry exists to inform it.

Automatic · cryptographic

Binding is guaranteed

A fulfillment is bound to your exact intent — intent ID, parties, asset, amount, expiry, and criterion commitment all sit in the verification request. A proof or preimage accepted for one intent can never be replayed against another. The core enforces this; you don't have to check it.

Human · judgment

Logic is your call

Whether a criterion releases only when it should is a property of its code. A buggy or malicious criterion could accept a fulfillment it shouldn't — releasing your escrow. The registry surfaces audits, verifiable builds, and track record so you can judge before you escrow.

Trust tiers

Five tiers.
Each earns its badge.

A tier is assigned from evidence, not opinion: public source, a reproducible build that matches deployed bytecode, and an independent audit. Friction in the console scales with the tier.

Official

In-repo, maintained by Laplace, audited, reproducible build. One-click in the console.

Audited

Third-party audit on file, verified build, public source. Trusted, with the report linked.

Community

Source public & build verified, but no audit. Usable — after you review the code.

Unverified

Only a program ID; no source, build, or audit. Heavy warning + typed acknowledgment.

Flagged

A known incident or deprecation. Hard-gated — the console blocks new intents.

Authoring happens out-of-band (deploy a program, or build an SP1 guest + vkey). Listing it here is a transparent, staged review — automated checks first, humans and auditors after. The tier is the outcome, not the input.
